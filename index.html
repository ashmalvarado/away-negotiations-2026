The core frameworks
Four concepts that separate reactive negotiators from strategic ones. Know these cold — they apply to every deal you'll ever do.
BATNA — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement
Your walkaway option. The stronger your BATNA, the more leverage you have. Before any negotiation, ask: what do I do if this deal falls apart? If the answer is "nothing good," you need to build alternatives first.
D2C example: Before renegotiating with your 3PL, get quotes from two competitors. You don't need to switch — but knowing you could changes the conversation.
ZOPA — Zone of Possible Agreement
The overlap between the lowest a seller will accept and the highest a buyer will pay (or vice versa). If there's no ZOPA, no deal happens. Your job is to find it — or create one by adding value on both sides.
D2C example: Your influencer wants $8K; your budget tops out at $5K. ZOPA may exist if you offer product equity, extended exclusivity, or a revenue share instead of a flat fee.
Anchoring
The first number set in a negotiation has disproportionate influence on the outcome. Whoever anchors first shapes the conversation. When you anchor high (as a seller) or low (as a buyer), you pull the final number in your direction.
D2C example: When a vendor asks "what's your budget?" — don't answer directly. Counter with "what's your standard pricing for a company our size?" Anchor only when you've done the research.
Positions vs. Interests
A position is what someone says they want. An interest is why they want it. Great negotiators move from positions to interests — it unlocks creative solutions neither side thought of.
D2C example: Your supplier's position is "we can't go below $4.50/unit." Their interest might be consistent, predictable volume. Offer a 12-month purchase commitment and you might get $3.80.
Bonus: The power of silence
After making an ask or receiving a counter, resist the urge to fill silence. Silence creates social pressure — and the person who speaks next typically gives a concession. Practice pausing for 3–5 seconds after any offer. It's uncomfortable at first. It works every time.
Prep like a pro
Negotiators who prep for 30 minutes consistently outperform those who rely on charm alone. Here's the pre-negotiation playbook — follow it before every significant conversation.
The 6-step prep process
1
Define your ideal outcome, target, and walkaway
Write down three numbers: what you'd love, what you expect, and your hard limit. Never negotiate without knowing your walkaway — or you'll drift past it.
2
Research their business
Google their company, read their reviews, check LinkedIn for tenure of their team, look at their pricing page. The more you know about their situation, the better you can find their interests.
3
Build (or strengthen) your BATNA
Get at least one alternative quote or option before the meeting. Even a weak alternative is better than none — it shifts your mindset from "I need this" to "I'm evaluating options."
4
List your tradeable variables
Price is only one lever. List everything that has value on both sides: payment terms, volume commitments, timelines, exclusivity, referrals, case studies, co-marketing. More variables = more flexibility.
5
Anticipate their objections
Write out the top 3 things they'll push back on and prepare your response. Nothing kills momentum like being caught off guard by a predictable counter.
6
Decide who speaks first
If you have strong market data, anchor first. If you're uncertain about their range, let them go first to reveal information. Have a plan — don't improvise this moment.
Pre-negotiation checklist
  • I know my ideal, target, and walkaway numbers
  • I've researched the counterpart's business
  • I have at least one BATNA identified
  • I've listed 5+ tradeable variables beyond price
  • I've written out their likely objections and my responses
  • I know who will speak first and why
  • I've scheduled the meeting (not rushed it at end of day/quarter)
Real D2C scenarios
Each scenario below is a situation you'll actually face. Read the setup, consider your approach, then reveal the playbook. Use these for role-play pairs in the session.
Vendor Your manufacturer just raised prices 12% ▼
You've been working with this supplier for 18 months. They've been solid, but just sent a new price sheet citing "raw material inflation." Your margin is already tight, and your CFO is watching. You have a call with them next Tuesday.
Your first move
Don't accept or reject over email. Get on a call. Ask them to walk you through the cost breakdown — "I want to understand where the increase is coming from." This signals you're engaged, not a pushover, and buys you time and info.
Tradeable variables
Extended PO commitment (6–12 months), faster payment terms (Net 15 vs Net 45), co-development of a lower-cost SKU, shifting some volume from them to a second source.
What to say
"We value this partnership deeply and want to make it work long-term. Help me understand what flexibility exists — if we commit to X units over the next 12 months, what does that do to the number?"
Agency Agency wants to raise your retainer 40% ▼
Your paid media agency has been with you 2 years. They're requesting a significant retainer increase citing team growth and scope creep. You've seen good results, but 40% feels steep. You don't want to start over with a new agency.
Your first move
Request a scope-of-work audit together. Ask them to itemize what they've delivered vs. what was contracted. Often this surfaces real scope creep — which is a legitimate basis for some increase — and gives you a rational anchor for a counter.
Tradeable variables
Scope reduction (remove lower-value work), performance bonus instead of flat increase, longer contract commitment for rate stability, case study rights, referral agreement.
What to say
"Before we talk numbers, can we align on what's in scope? I want to make sure we're paying for what we're actually using — and honestly, there might be things we should stop doing entirely. Then let's find a structure that works for both of us."
Creator Influencer quotes 3x your budget ▼
A mid-tier creator (280K followers, strong engagement, perfect demo fit) quoted $9,000 for a single sponsored post + story set. Your typical budget is $2,500–3,500 for this tier. You really want to work with them.
Your first move
Don't respond with just a lower number. Respond with curiosity: "Love your engagement numbers — before we get into pricing, tell me more about what you're delivering and what's driving your rate." You learn their priorities and find room to move.
Tradeable variables
Multi-post / longer-term deal, gifted product + affiliate commission structure, usage rights adjustment, exclusivity waiver, cross-promotion on brand channels, ambassador title.
What to say
"We're working with a tighter budget than that for this initial collab, but we see a lot of potential in a longer-term relationship. What if we structured this as a 3-month ambassador arrangement? That often makes the economics work better for both sides."
Platform Shopify Plus renewal — is there room to negotiate? ▼
Your Shopify Plus contract renews in 60 days. You're paying the standard rate and no one has ever questioned it. Your GMV has grown significantly — you're now a more attractive merchant. You're also casually evaluating other platforms.
Your first move
Initiate the conversation early — 60 days before renewal is the window. Request a call with your account manager and frame it as a strategic conversation, not a complaint. Reference your GMV growth as context for the ask.
Tradeable variables
Longer contract term (2-3 yr) for a lower monthly rate, credits for Shopify apps or services, migration support waivers, customized platform fee structure, added merchant services.
What to say
"We've scaled significantly with Shopify and we want to stay on the platform long-term. As we look at our renewal, we're also doing a broader vendor review. I'd love to understand what Shopify can offer us given our growth trajectory."
Words that work
The difference between "I'll think about it" and a deal often comes down to the exact words you use. Build fluency with these — then make them your own.
Opening moves
O
"Before we get into pricing, help me understand what's most important to you in this partnership."
Surfaces their interests. Creates goodwill. You learn before you commit.
O
"What does a typical deal look like for a company our size?"
Gets them to anchor — useful when you don't have strong data.
Anchoring
A
"Based on comparable vendors we've worked with, we'd expect this to land around $X."
Sets an anchor grounded in market data. Hard to dismiss.
A
"We've been quoted X by others — what can you do to earn our business?"
Introduces competition without being aggressive. Works well with SaaS vendors.
Push back without burning bridges
P
"I appreciate the offer, but I need you to do better than that for us to move forward."
Direct. Pressure without drama. Works in almost every context.
P
"I'm not in a position to say yes at that number. What flexibility do you have?"
Puts the next move on them. Avoids naming your counter too early.
P
"Help me get this approved internally — what else can you add to make this a stronger package?"
Classic "higher authority" move. Removes ego from the pushback.
Closing
C
"If we can land at X, I'm ready to move forward today."
Conditional close. Gives them a clear path to yes.
C
"Let's make this easy — can we split the difference at X?"
Only use this when the gap is genuinely small and you're both close.
Say this
"What I'm hearing is..." (mirrors their concern back)

"Is there any flexibility on..." (open, non-aggressive)

"Let me take that back to my team." (buys time, signals deliberation)
Not this
"That's way too expensive." (emotional, closes doors)

"Our budget is $X." (reveals ceiling immediately)

"We really need this done by Friday." (signals desperation)

Your negotiation starter kit
Leave today with three things: a mindset shift, a habit to start, and one thing to do before your next negotiation.
The mindset shift
Every negotiation is a problem to solve together — not a battle to win. The best deals feel good to both sides. When you approach it collaboratively, you get more creative solutions, longer-lasting relationships, and fewer reversals after the deal closes.
The habit to start this week
Before your next vendor call or rate conversation — any one — spend 15 minutes writing down your ideal, target, and walkaway numbers. Just that. It takes 15 minutes and it will immediately make you a better negotiator.
Your homework
Identify one negotiation you've been avoiding or accepting without question. Map out your BATNA, your tradeable variables, and your opening move. Bring it to the next peer session and we'll workshop it together.
